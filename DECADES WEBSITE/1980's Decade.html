HISTORICAL MOMENTS IN 1980S

WEDDING OF CHARLES & DIANA

Diana Spencer wed Charles, Prince of Wales, heir presumptive, at London's St. Paul's Cathedral on July 29, 1981. Additionally, the occasion was incredibly flamboyant. It attracted two million spectators to the streets of London, cost an estimated $100 million in today's currency, and was declared a public holiday in the United Kingdom. However, that pales in comparison to the 750 million people who watched from home.

The 1980 Moscow Olympics

The '80s were a great decade for the Olympics. Moscow '80, Los Angeles '84, and Seoul '88 each attracted roughly two billion viewers. But the Moscow games are historic in so many ways. They were the first Olympics ever to be held in Eastern Europe and the first summer Olympics ever to be held in a communist nation.

Accordingly, dozens upon dozens of nations boycotted the games and stayed away - 66, to be exact. Only 80 showed up, making this the smallest Olympics in almost 25 years.

JOHN LENNON ASSASSINATION

On the evening of 8 December 1980, John Lennon, the British singer and former Beatle, was shot and critically wounded in the archway of the Dakota, his New York City apartment building. On 14 December 1980, one week after the music icon's death, millions of people worldwide participated in 10 minutes of silence to honor Lennon at the behest of Yoko Ono.

It is difficult to exaggerate the legacy and influence of John Lennon 41 years after his death, and the fact that his presence remains today is evidence of this.

THE TIANANMEN PROTESTS(1989)

The Tiananmen Square protests, known in China as the June Fourth Incident, were student protests in Tiananmen Square, Beijing, China, from 15 April to 4 June 1989.
Repeated failures at negotiation over several weeks to achieve a peaceful resolution, the Chinese government moved troops into the square during the night of 3 June in what became known as the Tiananmen Square massacre. The protests are also referred to as the '89 Democracy Movement, the Tiananmen Square Incident, or the Tiananmen uprising.
1983 COUP

A military coup led by Major General Muhammadu Buhari ousted the democratically elected government of President Shehu Shagari. This marked the end of the Second Nigerian Republic. "This incident was one of the major contributing factors to the coup, as it placed the civilian government and military on opposite sides of a highly divisive issue." HISTORICAL MUSIC MOMENTS

MTV LAUNCH (1981)

MTV launched on August 1, 1981, becoming the first dedicated music video television channel.
It was owned by Warner-Amex Satellite Entertainment and initially featured music videos and related programming hosted by VJs (Video Jockeys). The launch occurred at 12:01 a.m. on Saturday,August 1st.
("MTV - Wikipedia")
MICHEAL JACKSON'S THRILLER (ALBUM)

Thriller is Michael Jackson's sixth studio album, released in 1982.
Produced by Quincy Jones, it features a mix of pop, post-disco, rock, funk, synth-pop, R&B, and darker themes.
The album features Paul McCartney as the first credited featured artist.
("live Aid Concert - Wikipedia")
LIVE AID CONCERT

Live Aid, a global concert organized by Bob Geldof and Midge Ure, took place on July 13, 1985, in London's Wembley Stadium and Philadelphia's JFK Stadium, attracting over 70 artists and bands and attracting 1.5 billion viewers worldwide.
THE RISE OF MADONNA

Madonna's path to stardom was a complicated one, beginning with dance in New York and later switching to music.
Her first album, Madonna (1983), was a success, but it was her second album, Like a Virgin (1984), that announced her arrival at superstardom and cultural significance.
DO THEY KNOW CHRISTMAS?

"Do They Know It's Christmas?" is a 1984 charity song by Bob Geldof and Midge Ure, written to raise money for the 1983-1985 Ethiopia famine.
Recorded by Band Aid, it reached number one in the UK singles chart and became the fastest-selling single in UK chart history.
The song sold 2.5 million copies in the US and 11.7 million worldwide by 1989 and 3.8 million in the UK by 2017.
EMERGENCE OF HIP HOP

Hip-hop's emergence in the early 1980s saw the genre solidify its identity, moving beyond its origins in the Bronx and spreading across the US and the world. This period was characterized by the rise of new school hip-hop, diversification of styles, and the burgeoning of hip-hop culture, including fashion, music videos, and film.

Fashion Details

POWER DRESSING

In the 1980s, power dressing emerged as a fashion statement for women seeking to assert authority and success in a traditionally male-dominated workplace. It involved sharp, tailored suits with wide shoulder pads, bold colors, and structured designs. This style was a way for women to project confidence and command respect, mirroring the styles of men in positions of power.
LEG WARMERS EVOLUTION

In the early 1980s leg warmers became a fad after David Lee and Trisha Kate opened a dancewear shop in the East Village, New York. They mainly sold legwarmers. Wearing them was fashionable among teenage girls; later to be an adopted fashion by boys in the city of Berkeley in the San Francisco Bay Area. Inspired by the fitness craze, leg warmers became a fashion statement
(Wikipedia,2024)
NEON FASHION

Neon colors emerged as a rebellious statement against the muted tones of the previous decades. Initially associated with underground and countercultural movements, these eye-catching, electric shades found their way into the mainstream. From the runways to street style, neon became a defining feature of the '80s aesthetic, symbolizing the era's unapologetic exuberance.
(Heirloom,2019)
ACID WASH JEANS

Acid wash jeans, characterized by their faded and distressed look, gained popularity in the 1980s and 1990s, with a resurgence in recent years. The process of acid washing involved using bleach and pumice stones to create a faded, mottled effect on denim, often associated with punk and rock subcultures. People dipped their jeans in diluted bleach and embellished them with metal studs, embroidery and rhinestones

Politics Details

RONALD REAGAN PRESIDENCY

Ronald Reagan's tenure as the 40th president of the United States began with his first inauguration on January 20, 1981, and ended on January 20, 1989. Reagan, a Republican from California, took office following his landslide victory over Democrat incumbent president Jimmy Carter and independent congressman John B. Anderson in the 1980 presidential election.
Reagan served two terms and was succeeded by his vice president, George H. W. Bush, who won the 1988 presidential election. Reagan's 1980 landslide election resulted from a dramatic conservative shift to the right in American politics, including a loss of confidence in liberal, New Deal, and Great Society programs and priorities that had dominated the national agenda since the 1930s.
MARGARET THATCHER'S ECONOMIC REFORMS

Thatcherism is a form of British conservative ideology named after Conservative Party leader Margaret Thatcher that relates to not just her political platform and particular policies but also her personal character and style of management while in office. Proponents of Thatcherism are referred to as Thatcherites. The term has been used to describe the principles of the British government under Thatcher from the 1979 general election to her resignation in 1990.In international terms, Thatcherites have been described as a part of the general socio-economic movement known as neoliberalism, with different countries besides the United Kingdom (such as the United States) sharing similar policies around expansionary capitalism.
FALL OF BERLIN WALL

The fall of the Berlin Wall on 9 November 1989, during the Peaceful Revolution, marked the beginning of the destruction of the Berlin Wall and the figurative Iron Curtain, as East Berlin transit restrictions were overwhelmed and discarded. It was one of the series of events that started the fall of communism in Central and Eastern Europe. The fall of the inner German border took place shortly afterward. An end to the Cold War was declared at the Malta Summit in early December, and German reunification took place in October the following year.
IRAN-CONTRA AFFAIR

The Iran–Contra affair, also referred to as the Iran–Contra scandal, the Iran Initiative, or simply Iran–Contra, was a political scandal in the United States that centered around arms trafficking to Iran between 1981 to 1986, facilitated by senior officials of the Ronald Reagan administration.
As Iran was subject to an arms embargo at the time of the scandal, the sale of arms was deemed illegal.

Tech Details

IBM PERSONAL COMPUTER

IBM introduced its first personal computer, the IBM 5150, in 1981. Equipped with a 4.77 MHz Intel 8088 processor and up to 256KB of memory, it set the standard for PC architecture and catalyzed the home computing revolution.

By 1984, IBM's revenue from the PC market was $4 billion, more than twice that of Apple.[48] A 1983 study of corporate customers found that two thirds of large customers standardizing on one computer chose the PC, while only 9% chose Apple.[49] A 1985 Fortune survey found that 56% of American companies with personal computers used PCs while 16% used Apple.
COMPACT DISK(CD)

The compact disc (CD) is a digital optical disc data storage format that was co-developed by Philips and Sony to store and play digital audio recordings. It uses the Compact Disc Digital Audio format which typically provides 74 minutes of audio on a disc. In later years, the compact disc was adapted for non-audio computer data storage purposes as CD-ROM and its derivatives. First released in Japan in October 1982, the CD was the second optical disc technology to be invented, after the much larger LaserDisc (LD). By 2007, 200 billion CDs (including audio CDs, CD-ROMs and CD-Rs) had been sold worldwide.
APPLE MACINTOSH

The Macintosh, later rebranded as the Macintosh 128K, is the original Macintosh personal computer from Apple.

It is the first successful mass-market all-in-one desktop personal computer with a graphical user interface, built-in screen and mouse. It was pivotal in establishing desktop publishing as a general office function. The motherboard, a 9 in (23 cm) CRT monochrome monitor, and a floppy drive are in a beige case with integrated carrying handle; it has a keyboard and single-button mouse. The Macintosh was introduced by a television commercial titled "1984" during Super Bowl XVIII on January 22, 1984,directed by Ridley Scott.
SONY WALKMAN

Walkman is a brand of portable audio players manufactured and marketed by Japanese company Sony since 1979.Walkman cassette players were very popular during the 1980s, which led to "walkman" becoming an genericized label term for personal compact stereos of any producer or brand.[6] 220 million cassette-type Walkmen were sold by the end of production in 2010;[7] including digital Walkman devices such as DAT, MiniDisc, CD (originally Discman then renamed the CD Walkman) and memory-type media players,it has sold approximately 400 million at this time.

The Walkman brand has also been applied to transistor radios, and Sony Ericsson mobile phones.

Sports Details

MIRACLE ON ICE(1980)

The United States men's ice hockey team shocked the world by defeating the heavily favored Soviet Union at the 1980 Winter Olympics In Lake Placid.
Consisting mostly of amateur and collegiate Players, coached by Herbs Brooks, and they pulled off a 4-3 Victory and earning the title of Miracle on ice.

This win was not only a monumental sports uproar but also a moment of national pride during a tense period of the Cold War. The U.S team went on to win the gold medal capturing the imagination of fans worldwide
COE VS OVETT RIVALRY (1980 MOSCOW OLYMPICS)

Sebastian Coe and steve Ovett stunned the UK with their legendary middle-distance rivalry at the 1980 Moscow games. Ovett, unbeaten over 1500m for years, took gold in the 800m while Coe, the 800m world record won a silver.

Their duels became a defining a moment in British athletics, inspiring a generation and providing a rare highlight during a challenging time for the UK.
WAYNE GRETZKY'S RECORD BREAKING ERA

Wayne Gretzky, known as "The Great One", revolutionized ice hockey during the 1980s. In the 1983-84 season, he scored a combined 100mgoals in a regular season and playoffs, leading the Edmonton Oiler to their first of four Stanley Cups in the decade.
Gretzky's achievements set new standards in the sport and expanded the NHL's influence internationally.
STEVE REDGRAVE'S FIRST OLYMPIC GOLD

Steve Redgrave began his journey to becoming Britain's most decorated Olympian by winning his first gold medal in rowing at the 1984 Los Angeles Olympics as part of the men's coxed four.
He would go on to win Five consecutive Olympic golds, but in 1984 his triumph marked the start of an unparalleled career in British Sport.
